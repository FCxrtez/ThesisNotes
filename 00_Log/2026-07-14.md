You’ve hit on the exact mathematical core of why high-precision golden models work. Your intuition is 100% correct: because the inputs are coming from a lower-precision format (`float16`), the higher-precision format (`float64`) has so much "room" that it can easily perform the intermediate arithmetic with absolutely zero loss of information.

Let’s break down exactly what happens bit-wise, explain why no rounding occurs during conversion, and walk through a formal mathematical proof.

---

## 1. Bit-Wise: Fitting `np.float16` into `np.float64`

An IEEE 754 floating-point number is split into three fields: **Sign ($S$)**, **Exponent ($E$)**, and **Fraction/Mantissa ($F$)**.

* **`float16` (Binary16):** $1$ Sign bit, $5$ Exponent bits (Bias = $15$), $10$ Fraction bits.
* **`float64` (Binary64):** $1$ Sign bit, $11$ Exponent bits (Bias = $1023$), $52$ Fraction bits.

When you cast a `float16` value to `float64`, **no rounding or approximation takes place**. The hardware performs a purely deterministic, lossless bit-expansion:

### The Bit-level Mapping (for Normal Numbers)

1. **Sign Bit ($1$ bit):** Copied directly.
2. **Fraction Bits ($10$ bits $\rightarrow$ $52$ bits):** The $10$ fraction bits from the `float16` are copied directly to the *most significant* $10$ bits of the `float64` fraction. The remaining $42$ least significant bits are padded with **zeros**.
3. **Exponent Bits ($5$ bits $\rightarrow$ $11$ bits):** The exponent is adjusted to account for the difference in bias:

$$E_{64} = E_{16} - \text{Bias}_{16} + \text{Bias}_{64} = E_{16} - 15 + 1023 = E_{16} + 1008$$



Because the target format has a larger exponent range ($11 > 5$ bits) and a larger fraction capacity ($52 > 10$ bits), every single representable value in `float16` (including subnormals, infinities, and NaNs) has an **exact, identical counterpart** in `float64`.

---

## 2. Why Intermediate `float64` Additions Don't Round

When we say "no rounding occurs during intermediate additions in `float64`," we mean that `float64` has enough fraction bits to hold the aligned mantissas of all four `float16` numbers simultaneously without having to drop any bits off the right side (which is what triggers rounding).

In your Tenca adder, when you align the four operands, you shift their mantissas relative to each other.

* If you do this in `float16`, you only have $11$ bits of precision at each step, so shifted-out bits are immediately rounded away.
* If you do this in `float64`, you have $53$ bits of precision. The shifted-out bits simply occupy the lower regions of those $53$ bits. No bits fall off the cliff!

---

## 3. Formal Proof: Why 53 Bits is "Infinite Precision" for Four FP16s

We can formally prove that the exact sum of any four `float16` numbers can be represented in `float64` with **zero rounding error**.

### Step 1: Define the Minimum Quantum (ULP) of FP16

The smallest positive non-zero value representable in `float16` is the smallest subnormal number:


$$\text{MinValue} = 2^{-14} \times 2^{-10} = 2^{-24}$$

Because of the binary grid of floating-point numbers, **every representable `float16` value** ($x$) is mathematically an integer multiple of this minimum quantum.


$$x_i = I_i \times 2^{-24}$$


Where $I_i$ is a signed integer.

### Step 2: Define the Sum of Four FP16 Numbers

Let $S$ be the exact sum of four arbitrary `float16` numbers:


$$S = x_1 + x_2 + x_3 + x_4$$

Substituting our integer representation:


$$S = (I_1 \times 2^{-24}) + (I_2 \times 2^{-24}) + (I_3 \times 2^{-24}) + (I_4 \times 2^{-24})$$

$$S = (I_1 + I_2 + I_3 + I_4) \times 2^{-24}$$

$$S = I_{\text{sum}} \times 2^{-24}$$


Where $I_{\text{sum}}$ is a single signed integer.

### Step 3: Find the Upper Bound of the Sum

The maximum absolute value representable in `float16` is $65504$. Therefore:


$$\vert{}x_i\vert{} \le 65504$$

The maximum absolute value of our sum $S$ of four operands is:


$$\vert{}S\vert{} \le 4 \times 65504 = 262016$$

### Step 4: Determine the Maximum Bits Required for $I_{\text{sum}}$

Since $S = I_{\text{sum}} \times 2^{-24}$, the maximum absolute value of the integer $I_{\text{sum}}$ is:


$$\vert{}I_{\text{sum}}\vert{} = \vert{}S\vert{} \times 2^{24} \le 262016 \times 2^{24}$$

Let’s find the next power of 2 that bounds $262016$:


$$262016 < 262144 = 2^{18}$$

Therefore:


$$\vert{}I_{\text{sum}}\vert{} < 2^{18} \times 2^{24} = 2^{42}$$

This tells us that the integer $I_{\text{sum}}$ requires **at most 42 bits** of precision to be represented with absolute, mathematical perfection.

### Step 5: Compare with `float64`

* The significand (mantissa) of a `float64` has **53 bits** of precision ($52$ explicit fraction bits + $1$ implicit lead bit).
* Since $53 \ge 42$, a `float64` significand can hold $I_{\text{sum}}$ **exactly**, without losing a single bit to truncation or rounding.

$$\text{Required Bits (42)} \le \text{Available Bits in FP64 (53)} \implies \text{Zero Rounding Error}$$

$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

*(Note: If you tried to use `float32` as your golden model accumulator, it only has $24$ bits of significand. Since $24 < 42$, `float32` **cannot** guarantee exact intermediate sums, and would introduce rounding errors before your final stage!)*

By performing the 4-operand addition in `float64`, you are calculating the mathematically perfect sum. When you finally cast that result back to `float16` at the very end, Python performs a single, mathematically rigorous IEEE-754 round-to-nearest-even step. This is identical to what your Tenca adder does in hardware using its $G, R, S$ rounding logic.
